The Science Behind DailyCalorie

The foundation of every calculation on DailyCalorie is the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, which is widely regarded by researchers and clinical nutritionists as the most accurate predictive formula for estimating Basal Metabolic Rate in healthy adults. Published in 1990 by Dr. M.D. Mifflin and Dr. S.T. St Jeor, this equation has been validated in numerous peer-reviewed studies and is recommended by the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics as the preferred method for BMR estimation.

Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR)

Your BMR represents the number of calories your body burns at complete rest — the energy required to keep your heart beating, your lungs breathing, your brain functioning, and your cells repairing themselves. It accounts for roughly 60 to 75 percent of your total daily calorie expenditure, making it the single most important number in your energy equation.

The Mifflin-St Jeor equation calculates BMR using four variables: weight, height, age, and biological sex. For men, the formula is BMR = (10 x weight in kg) + (6.25 x height in cm) - (5 x age in years) + 5. For women, the formula is BMR = (10 x weight in kg) + (6.25 x height in cm) - (5 x age in years) - 161. These formulas reflect the metabolic differences between sexes and the natural decline in metabolism that occurs with aging.

Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE)

While your BMR tells you what your body needs at rest, your TDEE accounts for all the additional energy you burn through daily activities and exercise. DailyCalorie multiplies your BMR by a carefully calibrated activity factor based on your lifestyle. Sedentary individuals (desk jobs, minimal exercise) use a multiplier of 1.2, lightly active people (light exercise one to three days per week) use 1.375, moderately active people (moderate exercise three to five days per week) use 1.55, very active people (hard exercise six to seven days per week) use 1.725, and extremely active individuals (very hard exercise, physical job, or training twice per day) use 1.9.

Your TDEE is the calorie intake at which your weight remains stable. To lose weight, you eat below your TDEE, creating a caloric deficit. To gain weight or build muscle, you eat above your TDEE, creating a caloric surplus. DailyCalorie presents both options alongside your maintenance level so you can choose the path that aligns with your goals.

Macronutrient Calculations

Calories alone do not tell the whole story. The composition of those calories — how they are distributed among protein, carbohydrates, and fat — has a profound impact on your body composition, athletic performance, satiety, and overall health. DailyCalorie calculates your macro targets based on established nutritional science:

  • Protein (4 calories per gram): Essential for muscle repair, immune function, and enzyme production. DailyCalorie recommends a protein intake that supports lean mass retention whether you are in a deficit or surplus.
  • Carbohydrates (4 calories per gram): Your body's preferred fuel source for high-intensity exercise and brain function. Adequate carbohydrate intake ensures you have the energy to train hard and think clearly.
  • Fat (9 calories per gram): Critical for hormone production, vitamin absorption, and cell membrane integrity. Dietary fat should never be eliminated entirely, and DailyCalorie ensures your fat target stays within a healthy range.

The interactive charts on the dashboard visualize these macro splits so you can immediately see the balance among the three macronutrients. This visual approach makes it far easier to plan meals and track your intake throughout the day.
